How to Choose the Right FRP Radome for Your Application

A practical guide to selecting fiberglass radomes for 5G, radar, and satellite communication systems.

May 28, 2026 8 min read

Selecting the right FRP (Fiber Reinforced Polymer) radome is critical for protecting your antenna system while maintaining optimal signal performance. This guide covers the key factors you need to consider when choosing a radome for 5G base stations, radar systems, or satellite communication terminals.

1. Frequency Band Compatibility

The most critical factor is ensuring your radome is optimized for your operating frequency band. FRP radomes can be designed for:

  • L-band (1-2 GHz): GPS, Inmarsat, air traffic control radar
  • S-band (2-4 GHz): Weather radar, satellite uplink, WiFi
  • C-band (4-8 GHz): Satellite communication, WiMAX
  • X-band (8-12 GHz): Military radar, weather radar, marine radar
  • Ku-band (12-18 GHz): Satellite TV, VSAT terminals
  • Ka-band (26.5-40 GHz): High-throughput satellite, 5G backhaul

💡 Pro Tip: Higher frequencies require tighter wall thickness tolerances. For Ka-band applications, specify wall thickness accuracy within ±0.1mm.

2. Environmental Conditions

Your radome must withstand the environmental conditions at the installation site:

Environment Key Requirements
Coastal/Marine Salt spray resistance, UV protection, IP65+ rating
Desert/Arid Sand abrasion resistance, extreme temperature cycling
Arctic/High Altitude Ice loading capacity, low-temperature flexibility
Industrial Chemical resistance, EMI shielding options

3. Radome Wall Construction

FRP radomes typically use one of three wall constructions:

Single-Layer (Monolithic)

Best for low-frequency applications (L, S bands). Simple construction, cost-effective, good mechanical strength.

Multi-Layer (Sandwich)

Skin-core-skin structure for X, Ku, Ka bands. Excellent stiffness-to-weight ratio, superior RF transparency.

A-Sandwich (Tuned)

Precision-engineered for minimal insertion loss. Used in high-performance radar and satellite systems.

4. Size and Shape Considerations

Radome geometry affects both RF performance and structural integrity:

  • Spherical: Omnidirectional antennas, minimal boresight error
  • Planar: Flat panel antennas, phased arrays, easier to manufacture
  • Cylindrical: Ground-based radar, satellite tracking
  • Custom: Conformal radomes for aerodynamic applications

5. Key Specifications to Request

When requesting a quote from an FRP radome manufacturer, provide these specifications:

  1. Operating frequency range (min/max)
  2. Maximum acceptable insertion loss (dB)
  3. Antenna aperture size and type
  4. Environmental requirements (IP rating, wind load, ice load)
  5. Operating temperature range
  6. UV exposure and service life expectancy
  7. Any special requirements (EMI shielding, de-icing, lightning protection)

Conclusion

Choosing the right FRP radome requires balancing RF performance, environmental protection, and cost. By considering frequency band, environmental conditions, wall construction, and geometry, you can ensure your antenna system performs reliably for years to come.
